Under Review

Performance and Environmental Assessment of Alginate–Synthetic Thickener Blends in Reactive Printing on Lyocell Knitted Fabric

Research Background

A few years ago, I started asking a simple question: can we make textile printing less wasteful without sacrificing quality?

The short answer? Yes. But the path wasn't straightforward.

I've always loved the feel of lyocell—it's soft, breathable, and made in a closed-loop process that already respects the planet more than many other fibres. But printing on it? That's where things get messy. Most printers rely on sodium alginate, a natural thickener that works beautifully but costs a lot and leaves a heavy footprint in the water. Synthetic thickeners give brighter colours and sharper prints, yet they stiffen the fabric and bring their own environmental baggage.

So I thought: why not blend them?

Together with my co-author, we tested dozens of combinations. We measured colour yield, penetration, fixation, fabric drape – and also looked at what goes down the drain: BOD, COD, pH, all of it. After months of lab work, one blend stood out: 50% alginate, 50% synthetic. It gave the best of both worlds – rich colour, good handle, and much cleaner effluent.

That finding still excites me. It means factories don't have to choose between a great print and a responsible process. They can have both.

I'm sharing this because the research is now under review, and I hope it nudges the industry in a small but meaningful direction. Not every solution has to be radical. Sometimes the most practical ideas are also the most sustainable.

If you work in textile wet processing or just care about cleaner production, I'd love to hear your thoughts. This is only the beginning.

In Progress

Predictive Modeling of Performance and Durability in E-Textiles Using Machine Learning: A Critical Review

Research Background

Most of my earlier research focused on textile chemistry, functional finishes, and material performance. But over time, I became increasingly interested in where the industry itself is heading. Textiles are no longer just materials—they're becoming intelligent systems.

That curiosity led me toward smart wearables and e-textiles, especially the growing intersection between textiles, electronics, and artificial intelligence. What fascinated me most was a simple but important challenge: durability. Smart textiles can perform incredibly well in labs, but real-world conditions are different. Fabrics stretch, bend, wash, and gradually lose performance over time.

Through this work, I'm exploring how machine learning can help predict degradation, failure, and long-term performance in e-textile systems before those failures actually occur. More than anything, this project reflects my transition toward emerging technologies and my interest in shaping the future of smart apparel, wearable systems, and AI-driven textile innovation.
